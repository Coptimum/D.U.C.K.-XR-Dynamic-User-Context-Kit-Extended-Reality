Source 1: Study on Heart Rate Visualisation Using Combination of Real Time Heart Rate Detection and Augmented Reality
Summary:
 The paper discusses the difference between heartbeat and heart rate. It reviews traditional and modern methods for measuring heart rate—such as manual pulse counting, stethoscopes, ECGs, and chest straps. However, none of these devices offer visualizations of how the heart beats inside the body. The paper proposes using Augmented Reality (AR) to visualize the heart's beating process in real time, allowing users to interact with a 3D heart model using features like scaling, moving, and zooming.
Goal:
To develop an interactive AR application that visualizes real-time heartbeats and provides health information, encouraging users to monitor their heart rate more frequently and better understand their heart condition.


Source 2: Assessment of Ergonomic Posture in Augmented Reality Environments Using Slouching Score 

Summary: Paper discusses using slouching scores to assess ergonomic posture in AR environment using the Microsoft HoloLens 2 with Xsens motion capture. Participants completed a lecture on biomechanics in an AR environment before performing various tasks. During task performance, a posture recognition system analyzed participant movement to determine if it was "safe" or "unsafe", giving feedback when it was deemed "unsafe". Posture analysis was conducted via recording participant movement and video recording of activity and combining the two for accuracy. Real-time feedback gave participants guidance to maintain a neutral spine and keep the center of mass "balanced", facilitating active learning and reinforcing ergonomic principles.



Source 3: Standing postural control during an upper limb reaching task in a mixed reality condition: analysis of center of pressure and lower limb muscle activities

Overview: This study examines how usage of a mixed reality headset monitor display (MR-HMD) affects balance and postural control during a dynamic movement of the upper limb in a standing posture.

Notes:
Used HoloLens 2 for the MR
Also used a depth camera and 4 additional cameras with built-in sensors for environmental sensing and detecting ambient light
2 infrared cameras for sight tracking in real-time at a frequency of 30 Hz and manipulation of objections in the scene by eye-movement
Used a force platform to collect center of pressure data
Recorded EMG from peroneus longus (PL), tibialis anterior (TA), gastrocnemius medialis (GM), and soleus (SOL) muscles of the right lower limb
Followed SENIAM guideline for electrode placement http://seniam.org/ 
Amplified and then band-pass filtered the EMG signals at 20 Hz - 2 kHz
Recorded the EMG activities with a sampling rate of 5 kHz
Obtained EMG activity with maximum voluntary contraction (MVC) in each muscle before the task (I think this serves as a reference point)
The integral EMG (iEMG) of each muscle during the task was calculated and normalized to the MVC

Their findings suggest that wearing MR-HMD leads to better stability than in physical environments that induce postural control depending on visual feedback. 

 
Source 4: Metrology-Based Design of a Wearable Augmented Reality System for Monitoring Patient's Vitals in Real Time 

Overview: This study is about using an augmented-reality to monitor a patient's vitals in real time during surgical procedures. This gives the surgical team a real-time visualization without them having to constantly look at the instrumentation. (so the surgical staff are the people wearing the AR equipment)

Notes:
An equipment control unit (ECU) collects the vital parameters from the operating room equipment, then a data collector checks for possible errors to ensure accuracy
Their ECU is a laptop
They used Epson Moverio BT-350 AR glasses
Optical see-through glasses, allows the user to see digital images overlaid to the real world environment
To acquire and transmit the info, they used MATLAB
Connection from laptop to AR glasses is wireless

TLDR they have other equipment that measures the vitals, and then the info is transmitted to the AR device which displays it to the user
We could do something similar, have the user wear a wristband (a lot of people already have fitbits, apple watches, etc.) and just have the wristband transmit the data to the VR headset and display it in front of the user
Using wristbands to measure vitals is probably also more accurate than trying to do it through the headset itself


Source 5: The Effects of Virtual Reality Games in Posture Correction Exercise on the Posture and Balance of Patients with Forward Head Posture

Summary:
Paper looks at the effect that implementing posture improvement exercises into virtual reality programs (games) has on posture and balance of people with forward head posture (slouching at neck such that head sticks out)
Notes:
30 participants perform posture correction exercises with/without VR games
measured neck joint range of motion and stability during this time
done using sway test and posture analysis (measure distance between acromion - top of scapula and earlobe)

Source 6: Walking Posture Correction Using Mixed Reality for Self Visualization | SpringerLink 

Overview: This study is about using mixed reality tech to recreate a person's gait in real time. The recreation is displayed as a hologram in front of the participant so that they have real time visual feedback on their posture.

Notes:
They had participants wear a wireless motion capture suit and a HoloLens 2
^not super useful for us b/c we don't have a motion capture suit, nor is that a realistic thing to ask/expect people to wear in their day-to-day lives
They found that their proposed system led to a reduction in head swaying and lateral difference in shoulder height when walking.
